UNDER CONSTRUCTION

This project explores the impact of simple modifications of the most commonly used school admissions procedure, the deferred acceptance mechanism, on the behavior and perceptions of applicants. In a lab experiment, I compare the impact of statically and dynamically eliciting applicants' preferences and whether or not applicants are allowed to express their intensity of preference for each school. I show that eliciting participants' preferences dynamically does not increase truth-telling. I find that perceived fairness is higher when preferences of students are dynamically collected and students can express their preference intensity. These results suggest that simple modifications to strategy-proof mechanisms can motivate truthful behavior in practice and perceived fairness may be a more comprehensive notion of fairness in school choice.
